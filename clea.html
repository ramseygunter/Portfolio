Project overview

Brief

CLEA is an iOS-based, mobile corneal topographer that can assess dry eye, keratoconus, astigmatism, corneal power, and more — all from the palm of your hand.

Problem

Breaking into the well-established medical field with an iOS-based medical device is likely to be met with hesitation.

Solution

Mix traditional medical language with modern, user-friendly design so the device feels familiar to industry professionals while remaining accessible to all.

Understanding the user

Interviews

I interviewed 1 Optician and 2 Ophthalmologists to better understand their education, day to day workflows, and how they conduct eye exams.

Desk research

I also conducted desk research about pain points eye care professionals, and doctors in general, had in their practices. While I referenced resources that pulled from experiences around the globe, I primarily focused on doctors within the United States.

Personas

From this research, I created personas to help the team better identify with the doctors that would be using this device.

Marketing collaboration

I shared the personas and pain points with the Creative Director so we could ensure the language used in marketing spoke to our target users. Specifically, we emphasized the mobility and affordability of CLEA over traditional devices and the quality of exam results being on par with existing technology.

Understanding the industry

Competitive analysis

I looked at electronic health records (EHRs) and medical devices, primarily corneal topographers, most commonly used in eye care practices, and studied the language they used within their interfaces.

Defining medical terminology

Researching definitions

We compiled a definition list of all relevant medical terminology referenced by the doctors I interviewed, language used in EHRs, and terms found in other medical devices. This list was a source of truth that the team frequently referenced when designing.

Accessible language

I combined the medical terminology with clear and simple language, replacing the more complex terminology with simpler words when appropriate, to ensure the product language was accessible to all — not just doctors.

Inclusivity

Research

We researched gender identities that existed within gender selection in EHRs today.

Gender

I expanded on the original binary gender options in the designs to be more inclusive and representative of all genders, while also aligning with EHR standards.

Patient privacy, security, & integration

Patient privacy & security

Patient privacy was a top priority for us, so we ensured patient information and data management within CLEA was HIPAA compliant. This included but wasn't limited to: encryption of data at rest and in transit, blockchain-ready architecture, unique user identification, session timeouts, automatic logoff after inactivity, and system generated Medical Record Numbers (MRNs).

Integration

We also ensured all data collected aligned with EHR standards and was ready for Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine (DICOM) and Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR) integration.

The UX Designer and I conducted multiple usability tests on the iOS interface and recorded any insights that would require further iteration.

Broader usability testing

Takeaways

Working on CLEA reinforced how critical precise, familiar language is in high-stakes environments. When the stakes are a patient's eye health, even small content decisions have real consequences for how professionals trust and use a product. I look forward to seeing the impact CLEA has on eye care professionals and patients around the world.
